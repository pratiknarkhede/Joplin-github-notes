## Inversion of Control (IoC)

**"Don't call us, we'll call you."**

&nbsp;

**<span style="color: #fafafc;">That’s the essence of IoC. It means</span> **the control of object creation and management is inverted** <span style="color: #fafafc;">—</span>**

**<span style="color: #fafafc;">instead of your objects managing their own dependencies,</span> **some external framework or container does it for you** <span style="color: #fafafc;">.</span>**

&nbsp;

Before IoC:

- Your classes were responsible for creating and managing their own dependencies.
- Your classes don’t care *how* things get created — only that they’re available when needed.

* * *

&nbsp;

## Dependency Injection (DI) – How IoC Works in Practice

DI is the **mechanism** through which IoC is implemented.

There are 3 main types of DI in Spring:

1.  **Constructor Injection**
2.  **Setter Injection**
3.  **Field Injection (via @Autowired)**

&nbsp;

### Constructor Injection – Preferred Way (Immutable & Testable)

![d0656e30c1e08791cab98a47867362d0.png](../../_resources/d0656e30c1e08791cab98a47867362d0.png)

&nbsp;

**Why use constructor injection?**

- Ensures required dependencies are present at construction time.
- Makes your objects immutable.
- Easier to test and reason about.
